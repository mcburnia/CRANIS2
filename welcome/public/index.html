1. The Problem

Europe has introduced two major cybersecurity regulations that are fundamentally changing how software is built, sold, and operated:

  • The Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) — any product containing digital elements sold in the EU must meet cybersecurity requirements, carry a CE mark, and maintain ongoing compliance. This applies to software vendors, IoT manufacturers, and anyone placing digital products on the European market.
  • The NIS2 Directive — organisations operating essential and important infrastructure must demonstrate security baselines, including supply chain risk management. If you buy software, you need to know that your suppliers are secure.

These two regulations create a connected obligation across the entire software supply chain. Vendors must prove their products are safe. Operators must prove their suppliers are safe. Neither can do it without structured evidence.

Penalties: up to EUR 15 million or 2.5% of global annual turnover (CRA); up to EUR 10 million or 2% of global annual turnover (NIS2).

Most software companies — especially smaller ones — do not currently maintain SBOMs, dependency inventories, vulnerability monitoring processes, or regulatory documentation. Without automation, this work requires expensive consultants, manual audits that become stale immediately, and complex spreadsheets that nobody maintains.

Engineering teams simply cannot manage this alongside product development. The compliance burden is real, but the tooling has not existed to make it manageable.

7. Marketplace & Trust Network

The CRANIS2 marketplace connects both sides of the supply chain in a trusted environment where compliance is demonstrable rather than declarative.

  • Software vendors publish products with compliance badges computed from real platform data — vulnerability scans, obligation completion, licence coverage. These cannot be self-declared.
  • Enterprise buyers (including NIS2-regulated operators) discover and evaluate suppliers based on verified compliance evidence — not marketing claims.

The network effect: As more vendors register products and maintain compliance evidence, the platform becomes a trusted supply chain directory. Each vendor's CRA compliance becomes discoverable evidence that their enterprise customers can reference for NIS2 supply chain requirements. More vendors attract more buyers, which attracts more vendors.

Over time, CRANIS2 can evolve into trusted digital infrastructure for software governance within the European market. Individual compliance tools can be replicated. A network of verified vendors with accumulated compliance histories is significantly harder to replicate.

9. Regulatory Coverage

CRANIS2 launches with comprehensive CRA support and is designed to extend coverage as the regulatory landscape evolves.

RegulationCoverageDetail
Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) Full All 19 obligations, Annex VII technical file, EU DoC, ENISA Article 14 reporting, CRA category classification, CE marking readiness
NIS2 Directive Supply chain Supplier due diligence, dependency governance, vulnerability evidence — supports NIS2 supply chain risk management requirements (Article 21)
Radio Equipment Directive (RED) Roadmap Delegated acts on cybersecurity (Article 3.3 d/e/f) share requirements with CRA — existing CRA evidence provides significant coverage
AI Act Roadmap High-risk AI systems require technical documentation, risk assessment, and supply chain transparency — structural overlap with CRA compliance
Sector-specific Roadmap MDR (medical devices), DORA (financial services), and sector-specific cybersecurity requirements can build on the same evidence base
The platform's architecture — obligations engine, compliance gap analysis, technical documentation, evidence generation — is regulation-agnostic at its core. CRA is the launch vehicle, but the same patterns apply to any regulation that requires structured evidence of software governance.
